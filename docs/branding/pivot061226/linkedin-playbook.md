# OpsFluency — LinkedIn Voice Playbook
> Pivot package 2026-06-12. Rob's LinkedIn is Phase 1's primary sales channel.
> These rules govern every post Rob writes as OpsFluency founder.

---

## The LinkedIn Reality Check

LinkedIn is where plant managers go when they're looking for a solution they can't quite name yet. They are not searching for "bilingual SOP software." They are reading posts about operations problems that sound like their operations problems — and deciding whether the person who wrote the post understands their world.

Rob's LinkedIn job in Phase 1: **Be the person who clearly understands their world.**

Not: pitch the platform. Not: explain what OpsFluency does. Not: run growth-hacking campaigns.

When a plant manager reads Rob's posts for three weeks and then has a near-miss that requires bilingual safety documentation, Rob is who they call. That is the funnel.

## Voice Rules — LinkedIn Specific

### The hook is everything

LinkedIn shows 2-3 lines before "see more." Those 2-3 lines determine whether anyone reads the rest. The hook must do one of:
- Name a specific problem they have ("Your supervisors are spending 17 hours a week re-explaining the same three procedures.")
- Drop a specific number they're curious about ("30% cost reduction without cutting revenue. Here's how.")
- Make a direct claim they want to verify ("Most workers don't quit for pay. They quit for competence.")

Do not open with: "I've been thinking about...", "Great news!", "Excited to share...", "Hot take:", or any question that lets the reader opt out without clicking.

### Short beats long. Always.

Maximum 5-6 short paragraphs per post. Aim for 3-4. Each paragraph: 1-3 sentences. White space is your friend — LinkedIn renders single-line breaks as paragraph breaks. Use them.

**Wrong:**
> In the complex landscape of modern warehouse operations, where organizations face mounting pressure from multiple stakeholders including regulatory bodies, workers with diverse linguistic backgrounds, and supply chain partners, the challenge of maintaining operational efficiency while ensuring comprehensive communication across language barriers has become increasingly critical to long-term business sustainability.

**Right:**
> Your floor runs on Spanish.
> Your SOPs are in English.
> That gap is costing you workers every 60 days.

### First person. Always.

Rob writes as Rob. Not "we." Not "our team." Not "OpsFluency has found." Rob is the brand. He earned the credibility. The posts should reflect that.

Exception: when referring to something OpsFluency the product does. "OpsFluency builds a company glossary that..." is acceptable. "We at OpsFluency believe..." is not.

### Numbers over adjectives

"17 hours" beats "many hours."
"30% cost reduction" beats "significant savings."
"Week 8" beats "early in the employment period."

If you don't have a specific number, don't make one up — make the point without one. But when the number exists, use it.

### No hashtag stacks

Two hashtags maximum, placed at the end of the post. Choose from:
- `#warehouseoperations`
- `#manufacturing`
- `#operationsmanagement`
- `#soptraining`
- `#workforcedevelopment`
- `#bilingualworkforce`
- `#oshatraining`

Do not use trending hashtags unless directly relevant. Do not use `#innovation`, `#leadership`, `#entrepreneurship`, or `#motivated` — those are not Rob's audience.

## Post Formats

### Format A — Problem Post (highest engagement, use 40% of the time)

**Structure:**
1. Hook: the problem in one or two lines
2. Expand: why the problem is worse than it looks
3. Diagnosis: the root cause (not the solution)
4. Close: one sentence that earns the follow

**Example:**
> Your Spanish-speaking workers aren't making mistakes because they don't care.
>
> They're making mistakes because the SOP is in English and they nodded through the training because asking questions felt embarrassing.
>
> That's not a workforce problem. That's a documentation and delivery problem.
>
> And it costs you a new hire every 60 days.

*[No product mention. No CTA. Just the problem, clearly diagnosed.]*

---

### Format B — Story Post (highest trust-build, use 30% of the time)

A specific thing that happened. Real scenario from Rob's career or a client engagement. One scene, one lesson, told straight.

**Structure:**
1. Set the scene in one sentence
2. What happened
3. What Rob did or what he noticed
4. What the lesson was

**Example:**
> At one facility I managed, we had a 60% Spanish-speaking workforce and English-only SOPs.
>
> We ran training. Workers signed in. Everyone nodded.
>
> Then a worker loaded a pallet the wrong way — the same wrong way it had been loaded for three months.
>
> When we went back and watched the training video, it was entirely in English. The worker had no idea what he'd agreed to.
>
> That's when I stopped calling it a training problem and started calling it a language access problem.

*[No product mention unless the story leads naturally to it. The lesson is the point.]*

---

### Format C — Number Post (fastest to read, use 20% of the time)

Lead with a number. Explain where it came from. Draw the operational implication.

**Structure:**
1. Number as hook
2. Context: where the number comes from
3. Implication: what it means on the floor
4. Optional: what to do about it

**Example:**
> 17 hours.
>
> That's how much time the average warehouse supervisor spends per week re-explaining the same three procedures to new hires.
>
> That's two full days of supervisory labor — every week — on content that should be self-service.
>
> The procedures exist. Workers just can't access them at the machine, in their language, without asking.
>
> That's the problem I built OpsFluency to solve.

*[Product mention is acceptable here because the number has already earned the right to a solution.]*

---

### Format D — Credibility Post (periodic, use sparingly — 10% of time)

Rob's results, told without ego. One result, fully contextualized so it doesn't read as bragging.

**Structure:**
1. Set up the situation (what was broken)
2. What Rob did
3. The result with the specific number
4. One-line takeaway

**Example:**
> When I took over operations at Nature's Distribution, they had good people and broken systems.
>
> We restructured the manufacturing and fulfillment workflows from scratch. Introduced cGMP standards. Fixed the receiving controls and lot coding.
>
> By the end: 30% improvement in efficiency and output. 30% cost reduction. Zero lost-time accidents.
>
> None of that was magic. It was SOPs that workers could actually follow, maintained by systems that didn't rely on one person's memory.

*[This type of post should feel like a case study, not a brag. The metric is proof, not the headline.]*

## Topic Buckets

Rotate across these topic areas. Hitting the same topic two weeks in a row is fine. Going more than a month without touching a bucket means the audience only sees one dimension of Rob.

| Bucket | What it covers | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **Bilingual workforce** | Language access, training friction, turnover caused by English-only SOPs | 2x/month |
| **SOP discipline** | How to write SOPs that workers actually use, SOP lifecycle, what makes a procedure stick | 2x/month |
| **Safety & OSHA** | Compliance documentation, proof of comprehension, near-miss prevention, what OSHA actually requires | 1x/month |
| **Turnover / retention** | First-60-days data, why workers leave, competence vs. pay as turnover driver | 1-2x/month |
| **Operations metrics** | Cost reduction, efficiency improvement, accuracy metrics from Rob's career | 1x/month |
| **Platform / OpsFluency** | What it does, how it works, specific features — only after earning the right with problem posts | 1x/month max |
| **Industry observations** | Manufacturing, 3PL, food/bev, fulfillment — what Rob is seeing across facilities | 1x/month |

**Ratio rule:** For every one OpsFluency post, write four problem/story/number posts. If the ratio drifts toward product content, the audience disengages.

## What Rob Does Not Post

| Never post | Why |
|---|---|
| "Excited to share..." openers | Signals content the writer cares about more than the reader |
| "Great news!" | Same |
| Reposts of LinkedIn news articles with "So interesting! Thoughts?" | Zero value. Sends zero signal about Rob's expertise. |
| Motivational quotes (his or others') | Not the brand |
| Platform feature announcements framed as news | Ops managers don't care about your changelog |
| Anything that requires reading a thread to understand the point | The post should stand alone |
| Employee appreciation posts at this stage | No employees yet; feels performative |
| "We're hiring" posts at this stage | Same |

## Comment Strategy

LinkedIn posts that get comments from the right people (plant managers, warehouse directors, EHS managers) are more valuable than posts that get likes.

- **Reply to every comment** in the first hour after posting — the algorithm rewards comment threads
- **Ask a follow-up question** in the reply — "What language breakdown does your floor have?" invites more
- **DM anyone who comments with a pain signal** — "We had the same near-miss last year" or "We're struggling with exactly this" — that is a warm lead, not a cold outreach
